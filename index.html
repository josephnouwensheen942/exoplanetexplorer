What are Exoplanets?

An exoplanet is any planet beyond our solar system. Most of them orbit other stars, but some are free-floating exoplanets, called rogue planets which are untethered to any star. So far, more than 5,600 planetsâ€™ existence has been confirmed. Most of the exoplanets discovered so far are in a relatively small region of our galaxy, the Milky Way. Even the closest known exoplanet to Earth, Proxima Centauri b, is still about 4 light-years away. We can determine the compositions of exoplanets, which range from rocky (like Earth and Venus) to gas-rich (like Jupiter and Saturn), by measuring their sizes (diameters) and masses (weights). Iron or carbon may be the dominant elements on some planets, whereas water or ice may dominate in others. Scientists so far, has discovered planets with dense cores that are still orbiting their stars, fluffy planets with densities comparable to Styrofoam, and lava worlds covered in molten oceans.
